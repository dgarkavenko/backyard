# Dead Relay -- Game Concept

**Working titles:** Dead Relay, Reserve Line, Last Tower West, Relay Night, Static Frontier

## Format

Single-player, first-person survival-defense with base maintenance, light crafting, and soft-fail progression.

## Core Fantasy

You are the operator of a forgotten reserve tower on a living frontier line. You are not here to win a war. You are here to keep one failing section of the system working for one more night.

The player fantasy is not power, but competence under pressure: inspect, patch, monitor, improvise, recover, and endure.

## High Concept

A first-person survival-defense game about loneliness, routine, and attrition. The player maintains a remote reserve relay tower on a secondary frontier line. The main line takes the brunt of the invasion elsewhere; only rare breaches reach this sector at first. But when they do, it means something upstream has already failed.

The tower is responsible for keeping a small section of the line functional: maintaining communications, preserving safe transit through a narrow corridor, and preventing hostile techno-organic life from overwhelming the sector. Waves do not attack simply to kill the player. They are trying to break the function of the outpost: blind the monitoring systems, damage the relay, corrupt infrastructure, and force open a path deeper into the line.

The player is fragile and underpowered. Success comes from preparation, observation, adaptation, and efficient recovery. Failure is rarely immediate. A bad night does not always end the game, but it leaves the outpost weaker, poorer, and harder to defend the next time.

## Setting

A remote sci-fi frontier where an aging network of towers maintains fragmented control over dangerous territory. The player's tower sits on a reserve line: an old secondary route used for emergency communications and occasional transit when the main line is compromised.

The playable space is intentionally tight:

- the tower interior
- its immediate perimeter
- a small exposed outer zone
- a narrow tunnel or service corridor leading toward one neighboring node

This keeps the game grounded, readable, and low-scope, while implying a much larger network beyond the playable map through radio chatter, distant lights, disrupted signals, and occasional transit events.

## What the Player Protects

The player is protecting the function of the sector, not just a building.

That function includes:

- the relay system that keeps communication alive along the reserve line
- the monitoring network that detects breaches and tracks movement
- the transit corridor that allows occasional critical shipments to pass
- the integrity of the perimeter, preventing the sector from becoming compromised ground

This creates meaningful pressure without requiring a giant world simulation.

## What the Waves Want

The hostile waves are techno-organic entities that slip through failures in the main line and spread through weak points in the network. Their goal is not primarily to hunt the operator. Their goal is to collapse the sector.

They do this by:

- attacking sensors, cables, relays, and exposed systems
- damaging barriers, turrets, and utility nodes
- corrupting parts of the environment
- breaching the transit route
- forcing the tower into manual operation
- opening a path further down the reserve line

This gives the enemy a clear ecological and systemic purpose.

## Core Gameplay Loop

### During preparation

- inspect and repair tower systems
- reposition monitors and surveillance coverage
- check likely breach routes
- scavenge scrap from previous encounters
- craft and place turrets, traps, mines, and utility devices
- decide how to allocate limited parts and limited time

### During pressure

- track incoming threats through cameras, sensors, and direct sight
- respond to changing breach patterns
- reroute your defense to match the current wave
- manually intervene when automated systems fail
- choose what to save and what to sacrifice

### After impact

- assess damage
- recover scrap and salvage
- triage critical systems
- restore partial functionality
- listen to radio traffic that reveals what is happening elsewhere on the line
- prepare for the next cycle under worse or altered conditions

## Pillars

1. **Loneliness and routine** -- The emotional texture of the game comes from repetition, maintenance, and quiet isolation. The player spends as much time preparing and checking systems as they do reacting to attacks.

2. **Vulnerability over empowerment** -- The player is not a frontline soldier. They are physically weak and logistically constrained. Direct combat is risky. Survival depends on planning and adaptation.

3. **Defense as maintenance** -- This is not about building a perfect fortress. Every defensive tool degrades, needs repositioning, consumes resources, or creates new blind spots.

4. **Soft fail and recovery** -- The game does not punish every mistake with instant defeat. Instead, failure compounds through damage, contamination, lost systems, and weaker future preparation.

5. **Mystery through infrastructure** -- The story is revealed through radio exchanges, system logs, interrupted transmissions, and changes in network behavior. The mystery lives inside the routine.

## Failure Structure

The game should avoid immediate binary failure unless the player has truly allowed the whole system to collapse.

### Local failure

The player survives, but loses something important:

- a turret destroyed
- a relay node disabled
- a sector left blind
- resources wasted
- transit delayed or damaged

### Sector degradation

Bad defense weakens the outpost over time:

- fewer supplies arrive
- some tools become unavailable
- more of the map becomes exposed or compromised
- wave routes change in the enemy's favor
- monitoring becomes unreliable

### Cascading crisis

The tower is still alive, but barely:

- systems must be operated manually
- defense coverage is patchy
- recovery takes longer than the downtime allows
- more risks are pushed onto the player directly

### Hard fail

Only when the sector can no longer perform its function:

- the relay goes dark
- the transit corridor is fully lost
- contamination becomes irreversible
- the line is officially considered broken

This structure tells the player: you are doing badly; prepare better -- without instantly ejecting them from the run.

## Main Resources

The most important resources are:

- **Parts** -- used for repair, crafting, replacement, and adaptation
- **Time** -- the true universal scarcity; every action means neglecting something else

Optional secondary resources:

- charge or power
- sensor coverage
- trap ammo
- salvage mass from enemy remains

The concept works best when the economy stays lean and understandable.

## Example Defensive Tools

- simple automated turrets
- proximity mines
- directional traps
- temporary barriers
- sensor spikes or portable cameras
- decoy emitters
- utility devices that stabilize systems rather than damage enemies

The interesting choice is not "what does the most DPS," but "what buys me the most control for the least maintenance."

## Progression

Progression should feel like increased competence, not superhero escalation.

Examples:

- new monitoring tools
- more flexible defense routing
- stronger recovery options
- faster repair workflows
- the ability to salvage specialized components from specific enemy types
- improved prediction of breach behavior

This keeps the tone intact: you become better at surviving deterioration, not invincible.

## Narrative Frame

The player is one operator in a larger but failing system. Through radio conversations and disrupted traffic, they piece together:

- what is happening on the main line
- why pressure is increasing on the reserve route
- what these entities really want
- what happened to neighboring towers
- whether command is still in control, or only pretending to be

The story should support the gameplay fantasy of isolation, ambiguity, and slow realization.
